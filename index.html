Why Designing?

My journey into design started with curiosity but it evolved into a passion for solving complex problems through structured systems.

What began as exploring how websites worked soon became an obsession with clarity, hierarchy, and user decision-making. I realized design wasn’t just about visuals, it was about shaping behavior, reducing friction, and aligning user goals with business objectives.

Over the years, that curiosity transformed into a focus on enterprise systems, workflow-heavy platforms, and conversion-driven experiences. I enjoy designing products where clarity reduces cognitive load, permissions prevent costly errors, and structure improves scalability.

Design, for me, is not decoration — it’s decision architecture.

Here is more about the WHY


I was feeling inspired by how modern photo-sharing platforms handle large, diverse collections of images. I wanted to use this inspiration to deepen my skills in visual hierarchy and complex mobile interactions. The core challenge I set for myself was: How do you design an "Explore" screen that feels dynamic, and how do you transition into a detail screen without the user losing context?

I decided to build a high-fidelity prototype of a photo inspiration app. The biggest learning goal for me was mastering the screen transitions and micro-interactions,specifically, making the transition from the image grid to the single photo view feel seamless and polished.

I truly believe a well-rounded UX/UI designer needs strong proficiency in motion and interaction design to deliver a holistic and delightful experience. This project was my focused effort to practice and prove those capabilities!


Here is more about the HOW


This was a project dedicated purely to visual design and prototyping. And not a full UX case study. So I intentionally skipped formal user-research steps like interviews, observations, and usability testing (which would be mandatory for a real product!).

I began with messy pen-and-paper wireframes to quickly define the grid, header, and layout. Then I moved straight into Figma to build high-fidelity screens, focusing on visual clarity, hierarchy, and seamless transitions.

It was a solo effort to push my production skills in Figma. Not perfect, but I'm proud of the overall fluidity and polish!

Challenges & How I Overcame Them

Turning obstacles into opportunities through creativity, calm, and clarity.

Working directly with marketing VPs, designers, and developers meant dealing with a lot of dynamic requests, often requiring out-of-the-box implementations. One recurring challenge was post-live issues due to clients not performing a hard refresh after deployment.

I remember one incident where a page went live, and soon after, there was panic. The client raised concerns saying "everything looks broken," with misaligned elements and visuals not rendering correctly. I stayed calm, assessed the situation quickly, and explained to both the team and the client that the issue was due to cached data and could be fixed with a hard refresh. The problem was resolved immediately, and I was appreciated for my timely response and clear communication.

Even if something was beyond what the designers could implement, I would guide them and explain, “You can actually do this and that.” I made sure they knew what was possible and how to approach it, helping turn ideas into reality even when they felt out of scope.

Another challenge I faced was when the development heads asked me to research a backend-related topic for the entire site. They said, "You can help with this." I had never worked on backend tasks before, but I didn’t say no. Instead, I saw it as a sign that they trusted my thinking and valued my input.

I spent time diving into the topic, did thorough research, and created a clear, concise report outlining the issue and how it could be solved. I overcame the challenge by believing in myself and the quality of my work. I was later appreciated for the detailed and thoughtful research I presented.
